Euler's identity is often called the most beautiful equation in mathematics:

$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

It connects five fundamental constants: $e$ (the base of natural logarithms), $i$ (the imaginary unit), $\pi$ (the ratio of circumference to diameter), $1$ (the multiplicative identity), and $0$ (the additive identity).

## Where Does It Come From?

The identity is a special case of **Euler's formula**, which states that for any real number $\theta$:

$$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

Substituting $\theta = \pi$:

$$e^{i\pi} = \cos\pi + i\sin\pi = -1 + 0 = -1$$

And therefore $e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$.

## The Taylor Series Proof

The cleanest way to prove Euler's formula is via Taylor series. Recall:

$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

Substituting $x = i\theta$ and separating real and imaginary parts (using $i^2=-1$, $i^3=-i$, $i^4=1$, …) gives exactly $\cos\theta + i\sin\theta$.

## Geometric Intuition

On the complex plane, $e^{i\theta}$ traces the **unit circle** as $\theta$ varies.
Setting $\theta = \pi$ rotates by 180°, landing at $-1$.

> "Like a piece of art, the identity can be appreciated without understanding — but understanding it makes it even more beautiful."

## Why It Matters

Euler's formula is indispensable in signal processing, quantum mechanics, electrical engineering,
and number theory — the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^{-s}$ being one example.
