The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) is the central result connecting
differentiation and integration.

## Part I

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a, b]$ and define the accumulation function:

$$F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\, dt$$

Then $F$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$ and $F'(x) = f(x)$.

**Proof sketch.** By definition of the derivative and the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals,
there exists $c_h \in [x, x+h]$ such that:

$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\, dt = \lim_{h \to 0} f(c_h) = f(x) \qquad\square$$

## Part II

If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$, then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

For example:

$$\int_0^1 x^2\, dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3}$$

## A Worked Example

Compute $\displaystyle\int_1^e \frac{\ln x}{x}\, dx$.

Let $u = \ln x$, $du = dx/x$. When $x=1$, $u=0$; when $x=e$, $u=1$:

$$\int_0^1 u\, du = \left[\frac{u^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}$$
