Given a square matrix $A$, a non-zero vector $\mathbf{v}$ is an **eigenvector** with
**eigenvalue** $\lambda$ if:

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$$

The transformation simply *scales* $\mathbf{v}$ — it doesn't rotate it off its span.

## Finding Eigenvalues

Rearranging: $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$. For a non-trivial solution to exist:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

For the matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$:

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$$

So $\lambda = 5$ or $\lambda = 2$.

## Finding Eigenvectors

For $\lambda = 5$: solve $(A - 5I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, giving $\mathbf{v} = t\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.

For $\lambda = 2$: $\mathbf{v} = t\begin{pmatrix}1\\-2\end{pmatrix}$.

## The Spectral Theorem

For a **symmetric** matrix ($A = A^T$), eigenvalues are always real and eigenvectors for
distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal — the foundation of **PCA**.

## PageRank

Google's original PageRank finds the dominant eigenvector of a Markov transition matrix:

$$\mathbf{r} = M\mathbf{r}$$

where $M_{ij}$ is the probability of following a link from page $j$ to page $i$.
