The **Basel problem** asks for the exact value of:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{16} + \cdots$$

## Euler's 1734 Proof

Euler treated $\sin x$ as an infinite product over its zeros:

$$\frac{\sin x}{x} = \prod_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{n^2\pi^2}\right)$$

Expanding and matching the $x^2$ coefficient against the Taylor series $\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{6} + \cdots$ gives:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$$

## A Rigorous Proof via Parseval's Theorem

The Fourier series of $f(x) = x$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$ is $x = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nx)$.

Applying **Parseval's identity**:

$$\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} x^2\,dx = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{4}{n^2}$$

$$\frac{2\pi^2}{3} = 8\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}$$

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6} \qquad\square$$
